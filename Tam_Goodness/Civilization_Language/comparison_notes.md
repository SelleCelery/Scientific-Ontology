# Comparison Notes

## Purpose

This document summarizes the comparison between the three execution modes used in the Bridge Lite pilot:

- `plain`
- `basis_on`
- `bridge_on`

The goal of the comparison is not to prove a completed theory, but to clarify how differently the same source text is processed depending on:

1. whether basis files are referenced,
2. whether invariant kernels are explicitly handled,
3. whether bridge transformation is applied.

The source text used in this pilot is a deliberately high-density, multi-phase self-descriptive passage. It mixes cosmological assumptions, boundary theory, practical self-definition, creative method, and AI-assisted interpretation in a single textual flow. The test is therefore designed to check whether a model can preserve a coherent axis under noisy and heterogeneous surface vocabulary. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

## Scope of the current pilot

This comparison is limited in several ways.

- It uses a single test case.
- It uses a relatively rich basis corpus.
- It does not establish universal validity of the three-kernel decomposition.
- It does not yet include adversarial or meaningless inputs.

Therefore, the present comparison should be read as a pilot run and a methodological anchor, not as a final validation.

---

## Output regime

A key design decision in this comparison is that all three modes share the same destination regime:

> a generally shared explanatory regime close to common scientific, human, and social common sense.

This matters because it prevents the comparison from collapsing into a comparison of output worldviews.  
The basis files in `basis_on` and `bridge_on` are used as support for input interpretation and alignment, not as the final worldview imposed on the output. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

## Mode summaries

### 1. plain

`plain` does not use basis files and does not explicitly activate bridge transformation.

Its role is to clarify the internal structure of the source text itself. In the current pilot, `plain` produces a readable structural outline of the passage: root assumption, boundary concept, job definition, creative practice, presentation policy, and AI requirement. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Important observation:

Although `plain` is not supposed to perform explicit bridge transformation, the result still shows kernel-like extraction. This suggests that a general LLM may already perform bridge-like structuring implicitly during ordinary clarification. In other words, `plain` is not “zero-bridge”; it is better understood as an instance of implicit, heuristic bridge-like behavior. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

### 2. basis_on

`basis_on` references the basis corpus but does not explicitly run bridge transformation.

Its role is to align the source text with existing conceptual coordinates. In this mode, terms such as root, void, qualia layer, boundary membrane, Gödel frontier, and structural language are restated in relation to the basis vocabulary, while the final prose remains in a common explanatory register. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Important observation:

`basis_on` does not simply impose a theory system on the source. It stabilizes understanding by giving the text a conceptual coordinate system. However, it still remains close to implicit kernel handling, because the preservation target is not yet fully governed as an explicit invariant transport process. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

### 3. bridge_on

`bridge_on` references the basis corpus and explicitly activates bridge transformation.

Its role is to separate:

- what must be preserved,
- what may be transformed,
- what must remain unresolved,
- and how the text should be read without collapsing its phase structure.

In this pilot, `bridge_on` explicitly organizes the text around three kernels:

1. support for cross-domain structural integration,
2. definition / relaxation / observation of the boundary membrane,
3. phase-latent presentation with AI-assisted post-organization. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Important observation:

`bridge_on` does not merely restate the text more elegantly.  
Its distinctive feature is that it makes preservation criteria explicit through protected boundaries, forbidden readings, and audit terms. This turns implicit bridge-like behavior into a more reproducible and inspectable procedure. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

## Comparative reading

The current pilot suggests the following interpretation:

- `plain` = implicit / heuristic bridge-like structuring
- `basis_on` = basis-aligned implicit structuring
- `bridge_on` = explicit / governed kernel-preserving bridge transformation

This distinction is important. The present results do **not** show that bridge capability appears from nowhere only when `bridge_on` is activated. Instead, they suggest that ordinary LLM processing may already contain weak bridge-like tendencies. The role of Bridge Lite is to make those tendencies explicit, constrained, and inspectable. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

---

## Main findings

1. The three modes are distinguishable as different processing attitudes, not merely as stylistic variations.
2. Even plain clarification may perform implicit kernel extraction.
3. Basis alignment stabilizes interpretation without necessarily changing the output worldview.
4. Bridge transformation becomes distinctive when preservation rules, protected boundaries, forbidden readings, and audit structure are made explicit.
5. The current pilot supports the view that Bridge Lite is less an invention of “bridge ability” and more an externalization and intensification of latent LLM structuring behavior. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

---

## Limitations

The current comparison remains provisional.

- Only one source text was tested.
- The basis corpus is relatively rich and may strengthen `basis_on`.
- The three-kernel decomposition may not generalize to all text types.
- No pseudo-profound, incoherent, or adversarial texts were included.
- The present logs still show some overlap between implicit and explicit kernel handling. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

---

## Next steps

The next stage should include:

1. additional text types,
2. sentence- or paragraph-level kernel tagging,
3. basis strength variation,
4. axiom-profile variation,
5. evaluation metrics such as:
   - translation rate,
   - axiom conflict rate,
   - untranslatable residue,
   - forced projection risk,
   - filter effect.

These extensions would move Bridge Lite from a pilot comparison framework toward a more explicit bridge-control system.
