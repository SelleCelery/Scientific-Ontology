# Foundations of History-Field Communication Theory

## A Theoretical Model Describing Human Beings as Self-Re-Encoding Terminals and the Universe as a Bosonic Network

### Abstract

This paper proposes a theoretical model that redescribes human cognition, experience, meaning formation, and ethical interaction through a unified structural language drawing on communication theory, field concepts, and network representation. In this model, a human being is defined as a **self-re-encoding terminal** that retains an internal density of historical traces and boundary conditions, and that compresses and decompresses event flows through the interaction of **semantic potential** and **experiential flux**. The universe, in turn, is described as a **bosonic carrier network** mediating interactions among such terminals, with the whole represented as an interaction graph. By mapping meaning onto an electric-like field (E), experience onto a magnetic-like field (B), event flow onto a current (J), and the density of informational history onto (\rho_h), the model formulates cognitive transformation in a Maxwell-type conceptual framework. This allows memory, interpretation, misunderstanding, learning, ethical conduct, and personality transformation to be treated as different phases of a single communicative process. The present paper is not an empirical study, nor does it claim literal physical lawfulness; rather, it is intended as a foundational theoretical draft for future formalization and comparative analysis.

**Keywords:** History-Field Communication Theory, self-re-encoding terminal, semantic potential, experiential flux, phase transition, cognitive communication, ethical communication, bosonic network

---

## 1. Problem Setting

### 1.1 Background

Discussions of human understanding often draw, in fragmented ways, upon psychology, philosophy, information theory, social theory, and physical metaphor. Yet a unified descriptive system that integrates these vocabularies remains underdeveloped. In particular, although many attempts have been made to describe human cognition in terms of input, internal transformation, and output, there is still no sufficiently coherent framework for describing experience, meaning, ethics, relationality, and transformations of world-disclosure within a single conceptual language.

### 1.2 Problem Awareness

Human beings are not mere receivers of information. Whenever an external input arrives, it is interpreted, compressed, transformed, and re-emitted in light of accumulated histories, preexisting structures of meaning, repeated experiential loops, and relational boundary conditions. A theory of this process cannot rely on “information” alone. It must also incorporate meaning, experience, history, boundaries, delay, and noise as analytically relevant variables.

### 1.3 Aim of the Study

The purpose of this paper is to propose a theoretical model that redefines the human being as a communication-theoretical terminal and that represents the interaction between internal states and the external world in a unified vocabulary. More specifically, the model seeks:

First, to define the individual human being as a self-re-encoding terminal that retains historical density and boundary conditions while compressing and decompressing event flows through the interaction of meaning and experience.

Second, to describe the universe as a bosonic carrier network in which such terminals are embedded, thereby connecting intrapersonal processes and global structure within a single communicative framework.

### 1.4 Position of the Paper

This paper does not seek to establish a strict natural-scientific theory. Rather, it presents a model for structural description. Accordingly, the equations proposed below should not be read as literal physical laws, but as **conceptual mappings** designed to articulate the dynamics of cognition and interaction.

## 2. Relation to Prior Conceptual Domains

### 2.1 Relation to Communication Theory

Communication theory provides concepts such as transmission, reception, coding, decoding, noise, and delay. The present model adopts these concepts but revises their target: the human being is not treated as a passive endpoint, but as an active terminal that continuously re-encodes input according to internal historical structure. Human communication is therefore not a matter of accurate signal transfer alone, but of interpretive transformation.

### 2.2 Relation to Field Concepts

Meaning and experience are better understood not as static substances, but as distributed fields operating within and across individuals. In this model, meaning functions as a gradient-like field that directs interpretation, whereas experience functions as a loop-forming field that generates recurrence, habit, and embodied inertia.

### 2.3 Relation to Network Theory

Individuals do not exist in isolation. They are linked through language, bodily interaction, institutions, expectations, and affective climates. Hence any adequate model must address not only intrapersonal processes but also the structure of inter-terminal linkage. The universe is therefore treated here as the totality of such mediated interconnections.

### 2.4 Relation to Ethical Theory

Ethics is often framed in terms of norms, commands, or virtues. While useful, such formulations are insufficient for structural analysis. This paper instead defines ethics as a **non-destructive communication protocol** that transmits necessary information without illegitimately damaging the historical density or boundary conditions of the other terminal.

## 3. The Model

### 3.1 Basic Hypotheses

The model rests on three hypotheses.

**Hypothesis 1**
A human being is a self-re-encoding terminal that transforms incoming signals in light of internally retained history.

**Hypothesis 2**
Meaning and experience exist as mutually interacting fields, characterized respectively by gradient and circulation, and they mediate cognitive and behavioral updating.

**Hypothesis 3**
The universe is a carrier network mediating interactions among terminals, and its totality can be described as an interaction graph.

### 3.2 Definition of the Human Terminal

Let a human terminal be denoted by (T_i), whose state is given by:

[
T_i = (\rho_{h,i}, E_i, B_i, J_i, S_i, Z_i, M_i, \tau_i, N_i)
]

where:

* (\rho_h): density of informational history
* (E): semantic potential field
* (B): experiential flux field
* (J): event flow
* (S): sink capacity
* (Z): interpretive impedance
* (M): boundary membrane conditions
* (\tau): response delay
* (N): residual noise

### 3.3 Conceptual Definitions of the Variables

#### 3.3.1 Density of Informational History (\rho_h)

(\rho_h) denotes the density of accumulated experiential and informational traces within the individual. It generates interpretive inertia: the denser the historical accumulation, the more strongly new inputs are drawn into existing contexts and less freely reinterpreted.

#### 3.3.2 Semantic Potential (E)

(E) denotes the gradient that determines the direction in which input becomes meaningful. It governs valuation, salience, orientation, and the conditions under which relations of significance arise.

#### 3.3.3 Experiential Flux (B)

(B) denotes the circulatory structure formed through repeated experience. Habits, embodied learning, rumination, traumatic recurrence, and skilled familiarity can all be described as biases or intensifications within experiential flux.

#### 3.3.4 Event Flow (J)

(J) denotes the stream of incoming events reaching the terminal from the outside. It includes speech, gaze, touch, social expectation, institutional pressure, and contingent occurrences.

#### 3.3.5 Sink Capacity (S)

(S) denotes the total amount of processing, buffering, and retention a terminal can sustain at a given moment. Under overload, compression efficiency and decoding accuracy decline, and noise increases.

#### 3.3.6 Interpretive Impedance (Z)

(Z) denotes the resistance encountered when incoming signals attempt to pass into meaningful form. High (Z) indicates difficulty of reception, resonance failure, or defensive rejection; low (Z) indicates relative ease of transmission and uptake.

#### 3.3.7 Boundary Membrane Conditions (M)

(M) denotes the conditions that regulate what is allowed through, what is blocked, and what is admitted only in transformed form. Intimacy, shame, vigilance, role-consciousness, and defense mechanisms are all included here.

#### 3.3.8 Delay (\tau)

(\tau) denotes the time lag between reception and response. It includes delayed understanding, deferred affect, and suspended reaction.

#### 3.3.9 Residual Noise (N)

(N) denotes the error residue left after reception, compression, decompression, and output. Misunderstanding, friction, and irreducible discrepancy belong to this dimension.

## 4. History-Field Communication Equations

In this model, the interaction of meaning and experience is expressed through Maxwell-type conceptual equations.

### 4.1 Divergence of the Semantic Field

[
\nabla \cdot E = \frac{\rho_h}{\varepsilon_s}
]

where (\varepsilon_s) denotes semantic permittivity. This equation states that nonuniform historical density functions as a source of semantic potential. Regions burdened with dense historical traces generate stronger biases of meaning under equivalent input.

### 4.2 Closure of the Experiential Field

[
\nabla \cdot B = 0
]

Experience persists not as isolated points but as loop-like structures. Repeated episodes, embodied habits, and unresolved recurrences are thus modeled as circulatory formations.

### 4.3 Rewiring of Meaning by Change in Experience

[
\nabla \times E = - \frac{\partial B}{\partial t}
]

Changes in experiential flux alter the local structure of semantic orientation. In other words, new experience can rewire the direction of meaning itself.

### 4.4 Updating of Experience by Event Flow and Semantic Displacement

[
\nabla \times B = \mu_s J + \mu_s \varepsilon_s \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}
]

where (\mu_s) denotes experiential permeability. This equation indicates that external event flow (J), together with internal shifts of meaning, reconfigures experiential structure. Human beings are changed not merely by what happens to them, but also by how what happens is made meaningful.

## 5. Compression and Decompression Processes

### 5.1 Compression Operator

Incoming information is not stored as raw input. Rather, it is compressed through historical density, semantic field, experiential field, and boundary conditions:

[
C : (E, B, \rho_h, J, M, context) \rightarrow \lambda
]

where (\lambda) denotes a latent code. This latent code is not a raw record of events, but a folded trace shaped by the history-field.

### 5.2 Decompression Operator

Latent codes are later unfolded under new contextual conditions:

[
D : (\lambda, context', M, Z) \rightarrow \hat{m}
]

where (\hat{m}) denotes reconstructed meaning content. The same latent code may yield different reconstructions when decompressed under different contexts, membrane conditions, or impedances.

### 5.3 Definition of Noise

Let (m) denote the pre-compression meaning content and (\hat{m}) the reconstructed content after decompression. Then noise is defined as:

[
N = | m - \hat{m} |
]

Misreading, transmission failure, and interpersonal friction may be described as increases in this residual discrepancy.

## 6. Propositions

### Proposition 1

**Human cognition is not mere reception but re-encoding.**

Inputs are not passively admitted into the interior. They are transformed through historical density, semantic potential, experiential flux, and boundary conditions. Cognition is therefore an active process of reconfiguration.

### Proposition 2

**Memory is not storage but compression, and recollection is not retrieval but decompression.**

What human beings retain is not the event as such, but a latent code compressed through the history-field. Recollection is the contextual unfolding of such latent encoding.

### Proposition 3

**Misunderstanding is not simply informational deficiency, but a mismatch of decompression conditions.**

Since sender and receiver differ in historical density, boundary conditions, and interpretive impedance, structurally divergent decompression is to be expected.

### Proposition 4

**Personality transformation involves not only gradual accumulation but phase transition.**

When accumulated inputs, experiences, or tensions reach a critical threshold, decoding rules, membrane conditions, and impedance structures may be reorganized discontinuously. This reorganization is termed phase transition.

### Proposition 5

**Ethics is a non-destructive communication protocol.**

Ethical interaction consists in transmitting necessary information while refraining from illegitimately damaging the historical density and boundary conditions of the other terminal.

### Proposition 6

**The universe is not merely the background surrounding individuals, but the carrier network mediating interactions among terminals.**

Individuals exist not outside the world but as nodes continuously exchanging signals within a larger communicative totality.

## 7. Theoretical Position of Phase Transition

### 7.1 Continuous and Discontinuous Change

This model distinguishes between two forms of cognitive transformation. The first is continuous optimization within an existing protocol. The second is discontinuous change involving alteration of the protocol itself.

### 7.2 Definition of Phase Transition

A phase transition is any transformation involving one or more of the following:

1. updating of decoding rules
2. reconfiguration of boundary conditions
3. change in impedance structure
4. updating of network topology
5. expansion of receivable bandwidth through the addition of new circuits

Thus, approaching truth is not merely a matter of increasing information quantity. It is a state change in which previously undecodable signals become decodable.

### 7.3 Justice and Truth

Within this framework:

* **Justice** is locally optimal communication within a fixed protocol.
* **Truth** is a global resynchronization involving transformation of the protocol itself.

Justice, then, is extension within a stable order; truth is reconfiguration of the order.

## 8. The Universe as a Bosonic Network

### 8.1 Global Structure

Let the universe be represented as a network

[
\mathcal{U} = (V, L, \Phi)
]

where (V) is the set of nodes, (L) the set of links, and (\Phi) the global field mediating interaction among terminals.

### 8.2 Broad Interpretation of Carrier Mediation

In this model, “bosonic” should not be read as a literal claim about elementary particle ontology in the strict physical sense. Rather, it designates a broad class of mediating carriers: light, sound, bodily motion, gaze, institutional force, symbols, expectation, and ambient atmosphere.

### 8.3 Feynman-Diagram-Like Representation

The total field of interaction may be represented schematically in a Feynman-diagram-like manner:

* lines: signal propagation
* vertices: interaction points
* loops: self-reference, rumination, unresolved recurrence
* external lines: observable utterances, actions, decisions

This representation allows intrapersonal and interpersonal processes to be modeled within a common interactional syntax.

## 9. Modeling Ethics

### 9.1 Definition of Ethics

Ethics is defined here as a communication protocol that transmits necessary information while respecting the other terminal’s historical density (\rho_h), boundary membrane (M), sink capacity (S), and interpretive impedance (Z).

### 9.2 Conditions of Ethical Communication

Ethical communication must satisfy at least the following:

1. it does not violate the other’s boundary conditions
2. it does not impose overload beyond the other’s sink capacity
3. it does not inject unnecessary noise
4. it does not disregard the other’s decoding conditions
5. when disconnection is necessary, it proceeds by rectification rather than destructive rupture

### 9.3 Definition of Violence

Violence is defined as a mode of communication that disregards the other terminal’s boundary conditions and decoding rules, imposing non-consensual overwrite through high load, high noise, and destructive force. Physical violence is included within this category, but the category is not limited to the physical.

## 10. Discussion

### 10.1 Significance of the Model

The significance of this model lies in its capacity to redescribe memory, interpretation, misunderstanding, learning, personality transformation, ethics, and relational formation not as unrelated domains but as phase differences within a single communicative structure. It thereby opens a path from descriptive psychology toward a more formal and structural language of human understanding.

### 10.2 Theoretical Usefulness

The model is useful in at least three respects. First, it translates subjective phenomena into structural vocabulary without evacuating lived experience. Second, it redescribes concepts such as ethics and truth in terms of state change and communicative constraint. Third, it connects intrapersonal dynamics to a larger image of the universe within one theoretical frame.

### 10.3 Limitations

The model also has clear limits. First, the variables remain insufficiently operationalized. Second, the equations are conceptual mappings rather than empirical physical laws. Third, future work will need to establish connections with psychological scales, behavioral indicators, dialogue analysis, and network analysis if empirical traction is to be sought.

## 11. Future Directions

### 11.1 Refinement of Operational Definitions

The variables (\rho_h), (E), (B), (Z), (S), and (N) require more precise operational counterparts if the model is to be used analytically beyond purely conceptual exposition.

### 11.2 Mathematical Formalization

The threshold conditions for phase transition, changes in impedance, the accumulation of noise, and the addition of new circuits should be developed into more rigorous mathematical expressions.

### 11.3 Comparative Theoretical Positioning

The model could be clarified further by being compared with epistemology, phenomenology, information theory, complexity studies, ethics, and social theory.

### 11.4 Development as a Structural Language

Ultimately, the ambition of this theory is not merely to produce an isolated essay, but to develop a structural language capable of traversing meaning, experience, relation, and cosmological framing in a unified descriptive system.

## 12. Conclusion

This paper has presented the foundational outline of **History-Field Communication Theory**, a theoretical model that describes human beings as self-re-encoding terminals and the universe as a bosonic carrier network. Within this framework, human cognition is understood as a communicative process formed through the interaction of historical density, semantic potential, experiential flux, event flow, boundary conditions, and impedance. Memory is redescribed as compression, recollection as decompression, misunderstanding as noise, personality transformation as phase transition, and ethics as non-destructive communication.

The central claim of the paper is simple:
**A human being is not merely an entity that receives information, but a terminal that decodes the world through history and rewrites itself in the process.**
And:
**The universe is the communicative totality that mediates the interactions among such terminals.**
